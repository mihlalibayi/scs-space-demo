Rainfall Anomalies

CHIRPS Monthly Rainfall · Digital Earth Africa Sandbox

The Western Cape is one of South Africa's most important agricultural regions, home to wine estates in Stellenbosch, fruit farms in the Hex River Valley, and wheat fields across the Swartland. All of it depends on rainfall. This session uses satellite-derived rainfall data to measure how much rain actually fell compared to the long-term average, and where across the landscape those differences were most severe.

Data Source
CHIRPS
Climate Hazards Group InfraRed Precipitation with Station data
Spatial Resolution
5 km
Per pixel rainfall estimate across the landscape
What is measured
mm deviation
Difference from the long-term monthly mean rainfall
How rainfall anomalies are calculated
01Define the study area and time period. For the Western Cape, this means selecting a bounding box that captures the major farming zones around Stellenbosch, Paarl, and the Breede River Valley, then choosing a season of interest such as the winter rainfall window from May to September.
02Load CHIRPS monthly rainfall data. CHIRPS blends satellite infrared data with ground station records to estimate how much rain fell each month at a 5km resolution. It is available through the Digital Earth Africa Sandbox for the entire African continent going back to 1981.
03Calculate raw anomalies and standardised anomalies. A raw anomaly is simply this month's rainfall minus the long-term average for that month. A standardised anomaly goes further, dividing that difference by the standard deviation, which removes seasonal variability and makes dry years in different seasons directly comparable.
04Visualise zonal averages and per-pixel maps. A zonal average gives a single number for the whole region. A per-pixel map shows exactly where within the Western Cape the deficit or surplus was concentrated, which is critical for targeted farm-level decisions.
CHIRPS v2.0DEA SandboxxarrayStandardised Anomaly
Key Takeaway
A single rainfall total tells you how much rain fell. A standardised anomaly tells you how unusual that amount was. For a wine farmer in Stellenbosch, knowing that this season was 1.8 standard deviations below normal is far more actionable than knowing it received 180mm instead of 240mm.

Sentinel-2 and Landsat

Vegetation Anomalies

NDVI · DEA Crop Mask · 30m NDVI Climatology

NDVI — the Normalised Difference Vegetation Index — is the most widely used satellite measure of plant health. It compares how much red light plants absorb versus how much near-infrared light they reflect. Healthy, dense vegetation absorbs red and reflects infrared strongly. Stressed or sparse vegetation does the opposite. For the fruit and wine farms of the Western Cape, tracking NDVI anomalies through the growing season reveals where crops are underperforming relative to historical baselines.

Primary Sensor
Sentinel-2
10m resolution, revisit every 5 days
Backup Sensor
Landsat 8/9
30m resolution, used to extend the time series
NDVI Formula
NIR minus Red
Divided by NIR plus Red. Range from -1 to +1
From satellite bands to vegetation anomaly
01Cloud masking removes noise. Clouds are the biggest obstacle in optical satellite data. The DEA pipeline applies a quality mask to each image, keeping only pixels with clear sky conditions. This is essential for the Western Cape's winter cloud cover periods.
02The DEA Crop Mask isolates farmland. Not every pixel in the Western Cape is a farm. The Digital Earth Africa crop mask filters the analysis to agricultural land only, ensuring that vineyards, orchards, and grain fields are measured without interference from fynbos or built-up areas.
03A continuous time series is built by gap-filling. Cloud gaps and missing observations leave holes in the data. Smoothing and interpolation techniques fill those holes to create a clean, continuous NDVI signal through the growing season.
04Standardised anomalies reveal true stress. Comparing current NDVI to the DEA 30m climatology baseline shows whether vegetation is greener or more stressed than it normally is at this time of year, which is the direct measure of crop health deviation.

Key Takeaway
NDVI anomalies do not just show where vegetation is sparse. They show where vegetation is underperforming relative to what it should be doing at this point in the season. A vineyard in Stellenbosch showing a negative NDVI anomaly in February is a flag worth investigating before harvest.
SESSION 03
SMAP and ERA5

Soil Moisture

NCI THREDDS OPeNDAP · ERA5 Precipitation · Surface and Root Zone

Rainfall tells you what fell from the sky. Soil moisture tells you what actually reached the roots. The two are related but not the same. Water can run off hard soils, evaporate before infiltrating, or be locked in the upper centimetres while the root zone stays dry. For South African farmers managing irrigation across the Hex River Valley or monitoring dryland wheat in the Swartland, soil moisture data gives a ground-truth view of what the plant is actually experiencing.

Surface Moisture
Top 5 cm
Immediate response to rainfall, evaporation, and irrigation
Root Zone Moisture
0 to 100 cm
What plant roots actually access for growth
Compared Against
ERA5
Copernicus climate reanalysis for precipitation context
Soil moisture layers and data sources
01Surface wetness from SMAP. NASA's Soil Moisture Active Passive satellite uses microwave energy to measure how wet the top few centimetres of soil are. This data is retrieved through NCI's THREDDS OPeNDAP service and covers the whole of South Africa at near daily intervals.
02Root zone wetness reveals what plants feel. The root zone model extends the surface signal deeper into the soil profile, estimating the moisture available to crop roots. A farm can show wet surface conditions after rain while the root zone remains in deficit from weeks of prior drought.
03ERA5 precipitation provides context. Comparing soil moisture trends against ERA5 rainfall data explains why moisture levels changed. This helps distinguish between drought, evaporation, and over-extraction scenarios, each requiring a different management response.
NASA SMAPNCI THREDDSERA5 CopernicusOPeNDAPRoot Zone Model
Key Takeaway
Rain gauges measure what fell. Soil moisture sensors measure what stayed. For a potato farmer in Ceres or a wheat grower near Swellendam, knowing the root zone moisture status in real time is the difference between timely irrigation and a missed yield window.
SESSION 04
Sentinel-2 Time Series

Vegetation Phenology

xr_phenology · Bar Tin Tin, Nigeria · Seasonal Dynamics

Phenology is the study of the timing of natural cycles. For crops, it tracks when vegetation greens up, peaks, and senesces through the season. By measuring these timing signatures from satellite NDVI time series, it is possible to detect whether crops are developing on schedule, running early or late due to climate shifts, or showing signs of stress that could affect final yield. This session works with data from Bar Tin Tin in Plateau State, Nigeria, a mixed vegetable farming area where the seasonal crop cycle is clearly visible from space.

Study Location
Bar Tin Tin
Plateau State, Nigeria. Cabbage, potatoes, peas, and carrots
Key Metric
Phenology Stats
Start of season, peak, end of season, season length
Method
xr_phenology
Digital Earth Africa function for temporal statistics
The crop phenology cycle as seen from satellite NDVI
01The NDVI time series is smoothed and interpolated. Raw satellite observations contain noise from atmospheric effects and residual cloud contamination. Smoothing creates a clean seasonal curve that faithfully represents the crop growth trajectory.
02Phenology statistics are extracted using xr_phenology. The DEA function identifies the start of season (SOS), peak of season (POS), end of season (EOS), and total season length from the NDVI curve. These can be derived for a whole zone or per individual pixel.
03Per-pixel analysis reveals spatial variation. Even within a single farm, phenology timing varies. A per-pixel map shows which parts of a field green up first, where peak NDVI is lowest, and where the season ends early — all indicators of soil variation, irrigation coverage, or localised stress.
Sentinel-2 L2ADEA xr_phenologyhdstatsBar Tin Tin NigeriaPhenology Statistics
Key Takeaway
A crop that peaks two weeks late or ends the season three weeks early is telling you something important. Phenology from satellite data captures these shifts across entire landscapes, giving early warning of harvest timing changes or stress events that ground surveys would miss until it is too late.

01Load and display previously generated results. Rainfall anomaly statistics, NDVI monthly means, and soil moisture time series from Sessions 1 to 3 are loaded and reviewed together for the first time in an integrated view.
02Compare standardised anomalies across layers. Plotting rainfall and NDVI standardised anomalies on the same axis reveals whether vegetation stress is tracking the rainfall deficit, which confirms the causal relationship and rules out other explanations like pest damage or disease.
03Overlay mean monthly NDVI, soil moisture, and total rainfall. This integrated multi-layer plot is the core of the intelligence output. It shows how the three variables move together through the season and identifies the months or periods where all three deteriorate simultaneously, representing the highest risk windows.
04Phenology statistics complete the picture. Adding the zonal and per-pixel phenology outputs from Session 4 reveals whether the seasonal cycle itself is shifting. A drought that delays or suppresses the peak growing period has compounding effects on final yield that simple NDVI values alone would not capture.
05Identify areas needing intervention. The combined view flags specific farm areas or zones where multiple indicators are simultaneously below normal. These become priority zones for agricultural extension services, emergency irrigation, or food security programme targeting.
